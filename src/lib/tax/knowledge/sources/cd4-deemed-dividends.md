# Deemed Dividends — Overdrawn Shareholder Current Accounts

## What is a deemed dividend?

Under section CD 4 of the Income Tax Act 2007, if a shareholder's current account with their company is overdrawn (i.e. the shareholder owes more to the company than the company owes to them), the overdrawn amount may be treated as a deemed dividend.

## When does it apply?

A deemed dividend arises when:
- A shareholder (or associated person) has a debit balance in their current account at any time during the income year
- The company has retained earnings or current-year profits
- No valid shareholder salary or dividend has been declared to offset the drawings

## Key rules

1. **Timing**: The deemed dividend is calculated at the end of the income year, but the overdrawn status at any point during the year can trigger it.

2. **Amount**: The deemed dividend amount is the lesser of:
   - The peak overdrawn balance during the year
   - The company's retained earnings plus current-year profit

3. **Tax implications**: Deemed dividends are:
   - Taxable income to the shareholder at their marginal tax rate
   - Not deductible by the company
   - May attract imputation credits if available

4. **FBT alternative**: If the company treats the overdrawn amount as a loan, FBT may apply on the interest-free benefit instead.

## How to avoid deemed dividends

- Declare shareholder salary before balance date to offset drawings
- Declare dividends with imputation credits
- Make cash repayments before year-end
- Maintain a credit balance by ensuring salary/dividends exceed drawings
- Keep accurate records of all transactions through the current account

## Common mistake

Many owner-operators draw funds throughout the year without tracking the balance. If total drawings exceed the salary/dividends declared, the excess is overdrawn and potentially a deemed dividend. This creates unexpected tax liabilities.

## Best practice

Track the shareholder current account monthly. If the balance is trending negative, consider declaring interim salary or making a repayment before the balance date.
